# Transition Roadmap

The developer-to-PM transition is one of the most common career pivots in tech. It's also one of the most frequently botched because people treat it as a job title change rather than a discipline change. This roadmap helps you make the transition with your eyes open.

## The four phases

### Phase 1: Explore (months 1–3)
**Goal:** Confirm this is actually what you want before committing.

The PM role is genuinely different from software development. It's high ambiguity, high communication, and outcomes-oriented rather than output-oriented. Some developers love it. Some discover they were happier shipping code.

**What to do in this phase:**

- Shadow a PM at your current company for a few weeks
- Run a side project where you're the PM — build something with a team or take on a product responsibility
- Talk to 5–10 PMs about their actual day-to-day — not their job descriptions
- Read deeply: *Inspired* (Cagan), *Continuous Discovery Habits* (Torres), *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* (Rumelt)
- Do the self-assessment in [Self-Assessment](self-assessment.md)

**Honest question to answer:** After this phase, can you articulate specifically why you want to be a PM, and which aspect of the PM role excites you most?

---

### Phase 2: Build Credibility (months 3–9)
**Goal:** Develop PM skills and a portfolio of evidence — while still an engineer.

The best way to get a PM role is to have already been doing PM work. You build credibility by creating evidence.

**Inside your current job:**
- Volunteer to write the PRD for your next sprint's major feature
- Ask to join customer calls with your CS or sales team
- Propose and run a discovery session with your team
- Build a small dashboard to track your feature's adoption metrics
- Own the post-launch review for something you ship

**Outside your current job:**
- Launch a side project you actually manage through the full product cycle
- Contribute to an open-source project in a PM capacity (write issues as feature specs, manage a backlog)
- Start writing about product problems you observe — a newsletter, LinkedIn posts, or a private blog
- Find a startup that needs a part-time PM or product adviser

**Deliverables to have by end of this phase:**
- A sample PRD (real or for a product you use that has a clear problem)
- A portfolio project — something you took from problem to shipped product
- 2–3 customer interview notes with synthesis

---

### Phase 3: Make the Move (months 6–18)
**Goal:** Get the first PM role.

**Internal transition (preferred first path):**
An internal transition is usually easier than an external one because you have context, relationships, and a track record. Approach:

1. Identify a PM role or opening at your company
2. Have an explicit conversation with your manager about your interest — don't surprise them
3. Ask the VP of Product for an informational meeting to understand how internal transitions work
4. Demonstrate PM skills in your current role (Phase 2 activities)
5. Apply when a role opens, with your portfolio as evidence

**External transition:**
If internal transition isn't possible or desirable:

- Target companies where your technical background is particularly valuable (developer tools, infrastructure, technical B2B products)
- Position yourself as a "technical PM" — this is a real and sought-after niche
- Apply to APM programs at larger tech companies if you're within 2–3 years of college graduation (Airbnb, Meta, Google APM programs)
- Look for "founding PM" roles at startups — they often value technical backgrounds and tolerate less PM experience

**The interview cycle:** See [Interview Prep](interview-prep.md) for detailed preparation.

---

### Phase 4: First 90 Days
**Goal:** Build credibility in the new role without reverting to your engineering instincts.

**Days 1–30:** Listen. Understand the product, the customers, the team, the strategy. Don't propose changes yet.

**Days 31–60:** Identify one area where your view adds value. Make a small, visible contribution — a good PRD, a discovery session, a metric dashboard that the team finds useful.

**Days 61–90:** Take ownership of a complete product cycle, even a small one: identify a problem, validate it, define a solution, ship it, measure the outcome.

**The thing that kills first-year PMs:** Reverting to engineering instincts when under pressure. Jumping into implementation decisions. Taking over technical discussions. Don't.

## Timeline reality check

| Path | Realistic timeline |
|------|-------------------|
| Internal transition at current company | 6–18 months |
| External transition, strong technical background | 12–24 months |
| APM program (early career) | 6–12 months to application, 1–2 year program |
| Startup founding PM | Variable — can be faster if you find the right company |

These timelines assume active effort. Passive hope takes longer.

## What the transition actually costs

**Time:** Phase 2 takes real work outside your normal job. Plan for 5–10 hours per week.

**Comfort:** You'll be operating at a lower skill level than you're used to as an engineer. That discomfort is unavoidable and temporary.

**Income:** Junior PM roles may pay less than senior engineering roles at the same company. This is a real tradeoff that varies by company and industry.

**Identity:** If you've identified strongly as "a developer," this transition involves a shift in how you think of yourself. Some people find this easy; for others it's genuinely uncomfortable.
