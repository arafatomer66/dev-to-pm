## Scoring guide

| Total score | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| **80–90** | Ready to interview for PM roles; focus on interview prep and portfolio polish |
| **60–79** | Strong foundation; 3–6 months of deliberate practice to close gaps |
| **40–59** | Solid starting point; 6–12 months of PM-adjacent work needed before transition |
| **Below 40** | Early exploration phase; focus on understanding the role before committing to transition |

## Reading by score range

**Score 0–1 in Section 3 (Business Acumen):** This is the most common gap for developer-turned-PMs. Prioritize: [Finance & P&L](../07-business-skills/finance-and-pl.md), [Business Models](../07-business-skills/business-models.md), [Vision & Strategy](../03-product-strategy/vision-and-strategy.md).

**Score 0–1 in Section 1 (Product Thinking):** Start doing customer interviews immediately. Nothing substitutes for direct customer contact. Target: 10 interviews in the next 30 days.

**Score 0–1 in Section 4 (Stakeholder Management):** Take on a project that requires cross-functional coordination. Volunteer for something that puts you in front of sales or CS.

**Score 0–1 in Section 2 (Communication):** Write a PRD for a real product problem. It doesn't need to be implemented — write it to practice the craft.

## The honest version

The score helps you prioritize, but it doesn't fully capture readiness. Some things matter more than the total:

- **Do you have evidence?** A portfolio beats a score. Can you show a PRD you wrote, a discovery session you ran, a feature you owned from problem to outcome?
- **Do you understand why you want to do this?** "I want to have more impact" is not enough. What specifically appeals to you about the PM role? Being clear on this helps you choose the right type of PM role.
- **Are you OK with the discomfort?** The first year of PM work is humbling. You'll feel less competent than you did as an engineer. That's normal and temporary. But it's real.
