# Certifications & Resources

An honest guide to what's worth your time and what isn't.

## Certifications

### The honest take on PM certifications

Unlike engineering (where certifications can validate specific skills), PM certifications generally don't carry the weight that practitioners would like them to. Most hiring managers at strong product companies don't factor them heavily. What matters more: a portfolio of real work, demonstrable product thinking, and relevant experience.

That said, certifications can be useful for:
- Structured learning if you're self-directed about applying the concepts
- Signaling interest and commitment when your background is non-traditional
- Specific methodologies (Scrum-specific certs are more valued in Scrum-heavy orgs)

### AIPMM's CPPM / CPM

General product management credentials. Moderate recognition in the industry. Worth pursuing only if you're in a context where formal credentials carry weight (large enterprise, regulated industries).

### General Assembly Product Management

Bootcamp-style course, not a traditional certification. Provides structured curriculum and portfolio projects. More useful than a certification because it forces you to produce work. Not cheap. Worth considering if you need structured accountability.

### APM Programs (Associate Product Manager)

Not certifications — these are entry-level PM programs at specific companies. Google APM, Meta RPM, Microsoft PM Rotation, LinkedIn ROTPM, Airbnb APM.

**These are the real paths for early-career candidates.** Extremely competitive (2–3% acceptance rates at top programs), but they provide structured PM mentorship, real product work, and a prestigious brand.

Eligibility: typically final-year students or recent graduates (1–2 years post-degree). If you're a senior engineer, you've likely aged out of these programs.

## Books (worth reading)

**Start here:**
- *Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love* — Marty Cagan. The standard reference for modern product management. Read it in your first month.
- *Continuous Discovery Habits* — Teresa Torres. The most practical book on discovery and opportunity-solution trees. Deeply useful.

**Strategy:**
- *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy* — Richard Rumelt. Fundamentally changes how you think about what strategy actually is vs. what most people call strategy.
- *The Innovator's Dilemma* — Clayton Christensen. Foundational. Explains why good companies fail and how disruption actually works.

**Business and leadership:**
- *Zero to One* — Peter Thiel. Contrarian thinking about building genuinely new products. Useful framework, not a recipe.
- *Measure What Matters* — John Doerr. OKRs in practice, with case studies from Google and others.

**Discovery and user research:**
- *The Mom Test* — Rob Fitzpatrick. The best book on running customer interviews without biasing the responses. Short, practical, immediately applicable.
- *Escaping the Build Trap* — Melissa Perri. How to build a product-led org and avoid feature factory culture.

**For the business side:**
- *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* — Ben Horowitz. Honest about what running a product org actually looks like.
- *Traction* — Gabriel Weinberg and Justin Mares. Go-to-market channels — useful for PM understanding how products grow.

**Skip:** Books that are primarily about process (agile, scrum, SAFe manuals). The concepts matter; the certification prep manuals don't.

## Communities

**Lenny's Newsletter / Lenny's Podcast:** The highest signal product management writing and conversations available. Read the newsletter; listen to the podcast episodes relevant to your gaps.

**Mind the Product:** Global PM community, conferences, online events. More useful at mid-senior level than entry level.

**Product School:** More of a training provider than a community. Useful for their curated blog content.

**Y Combinator resources:** The Startup School content (free) has excellent product and business thinking, especially for B2B SaaS. The "How to Start a Startup" lecture series is worth watching even if you're not starting a company.

**Reforge:** Premium programs run by growth and product practitioners. Expensive. Worth it if your company will pay for it. More useful once you have 2–3 years of PM experience.

## Courses

**Pragmatic Institute:** Well-respected for technical product management and B2B contexts. Better investment than generic PM certifications.

**Product School courses:** Lower cost, broader topics. Quality varies — read reviews before committing.

**Coursera / edX product management specializations:** IBM, Google, and Duke have product management tracks. Structured and reasonable quality. Better for learning the vocabulary than for building portfolio work.

## The best "certification" you can get

A portfolio of real work: a PRD you wrote, an experiment you designed and ran, a customer discovery project you led, a feature you shipped and measured.

This beats any certificate because it's evidence rather than credential. When you interview, you'll be walking through your work with real product managers who can assess its quality. No certification substitutes for that.
