# Remote & Async PM Work

The PM role has always been about alignment and communication. Remote and async work changes the mechanics without changing the goal. This page covers what breaks in remote PM work and how to fix it.

## What breaks

**Informal alignment.** Office-based PMs pick up a lot of context from overheard conversations, hallway chats, and body language in meetings. Remote PMs get none of this. Small misalignments that would be caught informally in person compound into big misalignments over weeks.

**Team cohesion.** Trust between PM and team is built through accumulated interactions. Remote work slows trust-building. A new developer-turned-PM starting remotely has a harder ramp than someone who can have coffee with their team.

**Meeting fatigue.** Remote teams default to video calls for everything that would have been in-person, creating a heavier meeting burden. The reaction — fewer, shorter meetings — often cuts the wrong meetings and keeps the wrong ones.

**Spontaneous discovery.** Customer visits, in-office customer panels, informal conversations with CS in the break room — these don't happen remotely. Discovery requires intentional investment.

## What works better

**Async decision-making.** Written proposals with structured comment periods force more explicit reasoning than meetings where whoever speaks loudest wins. Introverts and non-native English speakers often contribute more in async formats.

**Documentation.** The discipline of writing things down — decisions, rationale, meeting notes — is good practice anyway. Remote teams that document well have better institutional memory than office teams that rely on tribal knowledge.

**Global talent.** Async-first culture enables hiring the best person regardless of location. PMs at remote-first companies often work with larger and more diverse teams than their office-based counterparts.

**Deep work.** No commute, less ambient noise, and fewer drop-by interruptions mean remote PMs can often get more focused strategic work done.

## Async communication principles

### The async update standard

Every significant decision, direction change, or new information should be written down and shared proactively. Don't wait for people to ask.

Format: who needs to know, what happened, what it means, what's next, who to reply to if you have questions.

Slack is not sufficient for important information. Important information lives in Notion, Confluence, or wherever your team keeps permanent documents — not in a Slack thread that scrolls away.

### Timezone-aware scheduling

If your team spans timezones, the default of "call whenever" doesn't work. Establish:

- **Core overlap hours:** The window when everyone is expected to be available for synchronous communication
- **Async-first outside overlap:** Decisions that don't need immediate input get written up and shared async; responses expected within 24 hours
- **Rotation for inconvenient time zones:** If a meeting time is always bad for someone, rotate it

### Decision documentation

For any decision that affects people who weren't in the room, write it up:
- What was decided
- Why (the reasoning, not just the conclusion)
- Who was involved
- What would change the decision

This prevents the "but I wasn't in that meeting" complaint from turning into a re-litigated decision two weeks later.

## Running effective remote meetings

**Only schedule what requires synchronous discussion.** Status updates, one-directional information sharing, and async decisions don't need a meeting. Save synchronous time for: decisions that need debate, creative work that benefits from real-time collaboration, relationship building.

**Structured facilitation matters more remotely.** In person, you can read the room and redirect. On a video call, people are easier to interrupt and easier to ignore. Use explicit facilitation moves: "Let me check — [Name], do you have a view on this?" and "We've been on this point for 10 minutes. Can we decide or is this needing async time?"

**Share materials before the meeting.** A document shared 24 hours before lets people come prepared. A document shared at the start of the meeting is a meeting where the first 10 minutes are people reading.

**Record and share.** Meetings with important decisions should be recorded (with participants' consent). Team members in different timezones or who couldn't attend can review the recording and comments.

## Remote discovery

Customer research doesn't require in-person visits. Video calls work well for:
- User interviews
- Usability testing (share screen, observe their behavior)
- Design partner sessions
- Customer advisory board meetings

What you lose: the ambient context of visiting a customer's office. You don't see the sticky notes on their monitor, the whiteboards in the conference room, or the chaos of their Monday morning. To compensate:
- Ask more "walk me through a typical day" questions
- Do longer sessions to build enough rapport for honest answers
- Ask customers to screen-share while they show you their current workflow

## Building team relationships remotely

As a PM, your effectiveness depends on relationships with engineering, design, and other stakeholders. These are harder to build remotely.

**What works:**
- Dedicated relationship-building time: virtual coffee, Donut (randomized pairing Slack app), social calls separate from work meetings
- Be genuinely curious about people's non-work lives — you can tell when someone is going through the motions vs. actually interested
- Visit in person when budget allows — a 2-day offsite can build more relationship capital than months of remote work

**The new PM problem:** Starting a new PM role remotely is hard. You can't absorb culture by osmosis. You have to be more intentional: set up 1:1s with everyone in your first 2 weeks, ask more questions than you think you need to, and share your thinking in writing so people can see how you reason.
