# Working with Marketing

The PM-Marketing relationship is one of the most important and most frequently strained in a product organization. When it works well, product and marketing create a flywheel: product builds something worth talking about; marketing creates awareness and demand; demand informs what product builds next. When it breaks, you get misaligned messaging, last-minute launch panic, and a mutual sense of "they don't understand us."

## PM vs. Product Marketing Manager (PMM)

In most companies above ~50 people, there's a distinction between:

**Product Manager (PM):** Owns what gets built and why. Accountable for product outcomes.

**Product Marketing Manager (PMM):** Owns how the product is positioned and communicated to the market. Accountable for go-to-market outcomes: launch success, pipeline contribution, win rate.

These roles are deeply interdependent. The PM knows the product deeply; the PMM knows the market and the buyer. The best product decisions happen at the intersection.

In smaller companies, the PM often absorbs PMM work. If that's you, this page covers both sides.

## What marketing needs from PM

**Early:** Context on what's being built and why — early enough to develop messaging, not two weeks before launch.

**Specific:** Who is this for? What job does it do for them? Why now? What are they doing today instead? A PMM can't write good messaging without this.

**Honest:** What's the actual differentiation? What are the limitations? PMMs who learn product limitations from customers rather than PMs end up in difficult conversations.

**Stable:** Messaging built on shifting product decisions creates wasted work and organizational whiplash. When the roadmap changes in ways that affect positioning, communicate proactively.

## What PM needs from marketing

**Market intelligence:** What are customers and prospects saying? What objections come up in sales cycles? How do they describe their problems in their own words?

**Competitive intelligence:** What are competitors announcing? How are they positioning? What are buyers comparing us against?

**Messaging feedback:** Does the positioning resonate? What language lands? What confuses people?

**Launch support:** Coordinated execution of launches so engineering work actually reaches users.

## The positioning handoff

When you complete a feature or product, you need to give marketing enough to work with. A positioning handoff includes:

- **Problem statement:** What problem are we solving, for whom, and why is it painful?
- **Key differentiation:** What makes our approach different from alternatives?
- **Target segment:** Who specifically will care about this most?
- **Proof points:** Data, beta customer quotes, metrics
- **Language to use:** How do customers describe the problem in their own words?
- **Language to avoid:** Technical jargon, internal names, things that confuse buyers

The more specific you are, the better the resulting messaging. "This is for operations teams at mid-market logistics companies who currently track shipment exceptions in spreadsheets" produces better copy than "this is for operations professionals."

## The messaging brief

A messaging brief (sometimes called a "positioning brief" or "launch brief") documents the agreed-upon messaging before it goes into any external material. It should be reviewed and approved by both PM and PMM.

Core elements:
- Target persona and segment
- Headline value proposition
- Supporting key messages (3 max)
- Proof points and evidence
- Competitive differentiation
- What to say; what not to say

Once a messaging brief is approved, marketing can execute across channels (email, ads, website, blog, social) with confidence and consistency.

## The launch relationship

Launches work best when PM and PMM operate as co-owners of a shared timeline.

PM owns: what's shipping, when, at what quality bar, what the product can and can't do.

PMM owns: how it's announced, to whom, in what channels, with what message.

Neither can succeed without the other. The most common breakdown: PM treats launch as a product concern and marketing as "the people who write the email." This produces last-minute launch scrambles that damage the relationship and underdeliver for customers.

A simple fix: add PMM as a required reviewer in your PRD process. When a feature is approved for development, PMM is looped in immediately — not when engineering finishes.

## Working with demand generation

Demand gen focuses on top-of-funnel — getting the right prospects interested in the product. PMs aren't usually directly involved, but there are moments of connection:

- When demand gen is running campaigns based on positioning that's no longer accurate (PM needs to update them)
- When campaign performance reveals what resonates (PM should be learning from this)
- When new acquisition channels open opportunities for PLG features (PM can build product-led acquisition flows)

## When marketing and product conflict

**Scenario:** Marketing wants to announce a feature that's 80% done. PM knows the 20% isn't trivial.

**Resolution:** Be explicit about what the 80% can and can't do. Offer to help craft messaging that's honest about the current state ("now available in beta") without over-promising. Resist pressure to announce capabilities that aren't real.

**Scenario:** Marketing wants the roadmap for customer-facing communication. PM knows commitments are risky.

**Resolution:** Offer to provide directional areas of focus rather than specific features and dates. "We're investing heavily in enterprise security capabilities in H2" is true and useful without creating a commitment trap.
