# Roadmapping

The product roadmap is a communication tool, not a project plan. This distinction matters because it changes what you put in it, how you present it, and how you handle the inevitable requests to add dates to everything.

## What a roadmap is (and isn't)

**A roadmap is:**
- A statement of your product strategy in time
- A prioritized view of where you're investing effort
- A communication tool for internal and external alignment

**A roadmap is not:**
- A delivery commitment
- A feature list with dates
- A contract with customers or sales
- A substitute for strategy

The most common roadmap failure: treating it like a project plan. When you put dates on everything and commit to specific features in specific quarters, you've created a compliance burden that will make you defensive and reactive for the rest of the year.

## The outcome-based roadmap

Traditional roadmaps list features. Outcome-based roadmaps list the *outcomes you're pursuing* and leave the feature solutions open to discovery.

**Feature-based roadmap:**
```
Q1: Dark mode, bulk CSV export, Slack integration
Q2: Mobile app, SSO, custom reporting
```

**Outcome-based roadmap:**
```
Q1: Improve daily active user retention by reducing activation friction
Q2: Expand into enterprise accounts by meeting security and compliance requirements
```

The outcome-based version is better because:
- It's honest — you don't actually know in January which exact features will move the Q2 metric
- It keeps the team focused on the problem, not the solution
- It allows the discovery process to influence the specific features you build
- It's harder for stakeholders to hold you to specific features you haven't committed to

The tension: sales and customers often want feature-level specificity. They want to know *what* they're getting, not just the problem you're solving.

## Now / Next / Later

The simplest roadmap format that works at most company sizes:

```
NOW (this quarter)
-----------------
• [Thing we're actively building]
• [Thing we're actively building]
• [Discovery happening on X]

NEXT (next quarter)
-------------------
• [High-confidence item, more defined]
• [Item contingent on NOW learnings]

LATER (6–12+ months)
--------------------
• [Strategic direction areas, lower confidence]
• [Bets we're making but haven't fully scoped]
```

The reason this format works: it's honest about confidence levels without hiding uncertainty. "Later" items are not commitments. They're signals of where you're headed.

**Presenting it:** Explicitly tell stakeholders that confidence decreases as you go right. "Now" items are committed. "Next" items are probable. "Later" items are directional. This sets expectations correctly and prevents the "you said X was on the roadmap" conversation six months later.

## Handling date pressure

You will be asked to put dates on your roadmap. Here is what happens when you do:

1. You make a date up based on incomplete information
2. Stakeholders communicate that date to customers or put it in sales pitches
3. Development reveals the date was wrong
4. You have an external expectation you can't meet
5. The PM is blamed

This is not hypothetical — it's the standard trajectory.

**How to handle date requests:**

For "Now" items, it's reasonable to give a quarter-level commitment: "We're targeting this by end of Q2."

For "Next" and "Later" items, resist dates: "We'll have better information on timing once we've completed the Now work and validated our approach."

When customers or sales push harder: "I want to be honest with you — giving you a specific date before we've completed discovery would be a guess, and I'd rather give you a reliable timeline than an unreliable one. Once we start building, I can give you a more precise estimate."

Some customers will respect this. Some won't. For the ones who won't, your sales team needs to set expectations — this is a conversation to have with them, not a reason to commit to dates you can't keep.

## Internal vs. external roadmaps

Most companies need two versions:

**Internal roadmap:** More detail, includes engineering effort, dependencies, who owns what, confidence levels, open questions.

**External roadmap:** High-level, outcome-focused, avoids specific feature commitments, no dates or only directional timeframes ("H1 2026"), focuses on value delivered to customers.

Never share your internal roadmap with customers or partners. The specificity that's useful for internal alignment is a commitment trap externally.

## Roadmap reviews

Run a roadmap review with key stakeholders quarterly (or more frequently in fast-moving companies).

Agenda:
1. What did we commit to last quarter and what actually happened?
2. What did we learn that changed our thinking?
3. Here's the updated roadmap — here's what changed and why
4. Feedback and open questions

The retrospective element (what did we commit to vs. what happened) is the most important part. It builds trust by showing you're honest about misses and rigorous about learning from them. PMs who only show the forward-looking roadmap without acknowledging past performance signal that the roadmap is aspirational, not real.

## When the roadmap changes

It will. The market moves, customers reveal new information, a competitor launches something unexpected, engineering discovers a technical constraint. Roadmap changes are not failures — they're the system working correctly.

What matters is how you handle them:
1. **Acknowledge the change explicitly** — don't quietly update the doc and hope nobody notices
2. **Explain the reason** — what changed? New information? Changed priorities?
3. **Communicate early** — stakeholders affected by the change should hear from you before they discover it themselves
4. **Update all versions** — internal, external, and whatever stakeholders have on file
