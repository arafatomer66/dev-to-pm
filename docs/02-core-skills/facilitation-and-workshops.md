# Facilitation & Workshops

PMs run a lot of meetings. The difference between a PM who runs effective meetings and one who runs expensive calendar overhead is facilitation skill. This page covers the PM meeting formats that matter most and how to run each one well.

## The facilitator's job

A facilitator's job is not to have all the answers. It's to create conditions where the group can make good decisions together. This means:

- **Designing the agenda** so the right questions get asked in the right order
- **Keeping the conversation on track** without being controlling
- **Surfacing hidden disagreements** before they become blockers
- **Closing with a decision** — never ending a meeting without clear next steps and owners

PMs who confuse "facilitating" with "presenting their already-made decision" run meetings where people feel consulted but not heard. The result: low engagement, decision resistance later, and a team that stops contributing ideas.

## Discovery workshops

**Purpose:** Generate and prioritize problem areas, user needs, or opportunity spaces with a cross-functional group.

**Who to include:** PM, designer, 1–2 engineers, CS/support representative (they hear customer pain directly), occasionally a sales rep or customer.

**Formats that work:**

### How Might We (HMW)
1. Brief the group on a user problem or context (5 min)
2. Each person writes HMW questions on sticky notes: "How might we help users X so they can Y?" (10 min silent)
3. Group shares and clusters by theme (15 min)
4. Vote on the most promising clusters (5 min)

HMW reframes problems as opportunities without jumping to solutions. It's particularly useful when a group is too solution-focused early.

### Problem Framing
Present 3–5 problem statements you've drafted. Have the group rate each on two dimensions: importance to users and strategic fit. Use a 2×2 to cluster them. Discussion follows.

This is more structured than pure brainstorming and works well when you have some data but need stakeholder alignment on which problems to pursue.

## Roadmap workshops

**Purpose:** Build shared understanding of the roadmap and get input before it's locked.

**Who to include:** PM, engineering lead, design lead, key stakeholders (sales, CS, marketing).

**Format:**

1. **Set context** (10 min): What's our strategic objective this quarter/half? What have we learned recently?
2. **Propose the roadmap** (15 min): Walk through Now/Next/Later with brief rationale for each item
3. **Structured feedback** (20 min): Each stakeholder gives feedback in a specific format — "I'd add X because...", "I'm concerned about Y because..."
4. **Open Q&A** (15 min): Surface dependencies, risks, and constraints
5. **Close with what's decided vs. what's still open** (5 min)

The failure mode in roadmap workshops: PMs present a finished roadmap and ask for "questions." This signals that the roadmap is final and feedback isn't really welcome. The room reads this correctly, says nothing, and then pushes back later.

Instead: present the roadmap as a *proposal* and explicitly invite disagreement. "Is there anything here that seems wrong or that I'm missing?" is more productive than "Does anyone have questions?"

## Prioritization workshops

**Purpose:** Get stakeholder input on competing priorities before making a final decision.

**Format: Forced ranking**

1. Write each candidate item on a card (physical or digital)
2. Give each participant a fixed number of "votes" (e.g., 5 dot stickers)
3. Everyone votes independently and simultaneously
4. Tally votes and discuss the top items and the bottom items

Dot voting produces a quick picture of where the group's instincts align and diverge. Discuss the divergences — they're where the most useful information lives.

**Format: Buy a Feature**

Give stakeholders a "budget" (e.g., $1,000 in play money). List features with costs that reflect their effort. Stakeholders spend their budget on the features they value most.

This exercise forces tradeoff thinking rather than wishlist thinking. When stakeholders run out of money, they have to make real choices.

## Sprint retrospectives (from the PM seat)

Retros are primarily an engineering team ceremony, but PMs who skip them miss a rich source of information about how the team is functioning and what process changes would help.

**PM's role in retros:**
- Be present as a participant, not a facilitator (the Scrum Master or tech lead typically facilitates)
- Contribute genuine feedback, including things the PM could do better
- Listen for signals about blockers, confusion about requirements, and communication breakdowns
- Take action on retro items that are in the PM's domain

## Running effective meetings (general principles)

**Before the meeting:**
- Write an agenda with explicit outcomes: "By the end of this meeting, we will have decided X"
- Share the agenda 24 hours in advance
- Make it optional for anyone whose attendance isn't necessary

**During the meeting:**
- Start with the outcome, not the agenda: "Today we need to decide X because Y"
- Timebox discussions — use a visible timer
- When the discussion goes in circles, name it: "We've covered this point a few times. Let me try to summarize what I'm hearing and check if we're stuck or if we can move forward."
- Separate idea generation from evaluation — don't critique ideas while you're still generating them

**After the meeting:**
- Send a written summary within 2 hours: decisions made, actions with owners and due dates, open questions for next time
- If someone wasn't in the meeting, the written summary should make them fully current

**The meeting that shouldn't exist:** Most status update meetings can be replaced by a written update. Save synchronous time for decisions, alignment, and creative work. A meeting whose entire purpose is "what did everyone do this week" is a symptom of a broken async communication system.

## Async facilitation

Remote and async teams often need decision-making without a synchronous meeting. Formats that work:

**Written RFC (Request for Comments):** PM writes a proposal document, shares it with stakeholders, and requests written feedback by a specific date. PM synthesizes the feedback and makes a decision. Better for non-urgent decisions where people need time to think.

**Async prioritization:** Post the options in Slack or Confluence, ask stakeholders to vote and comment by EOD. PM reviews and decides.

**Loom/video walkthroughs:** Record a walkthrough of a spec or roadmap with narration. Stakeholders can watch asynchronously and comment. Particularly effective for visual artifacts.

The key to async facilitation: design the input format carefully. "Please review this doc and give feedback" produces scattered, unusable responses. "Please answer these three specific questions by Thursday" produces actionable input.
