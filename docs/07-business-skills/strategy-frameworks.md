# Strategy Frameworks

Strategy frameworks are thinking tools, not recipes. They help you structure analysis and surface questions you might not have asked. Use them to think; don't use them to replace thinking.

## Porter's Five Forces

Useful for: understanding the competitive dynamics of a market you're entering or defending

The five forces that determine industry profitability:

1. **Competitive rivalry:** How intense is competition among existing players?
2. **Threat of new entrants:** How easy is it for new competitors to enter?
3. **Threat of substitutes:** Can customers get the same job done a different way?
4. **Bargaining power of buyers:** Can customers demand lower prices or better terms?
5. **Bargaining power of suppliers:** Can vendors squeeze your margins?

**PM application:** Run this analysis when evaluating a new market segment or when your competitive position is shifting. A market with high competitive rivalry + low switching costs + easy entry + strong buyer power = difficult to build durable margins. A market with low rivalry + high switching costs + hard entry = strong position once established.

## Blue Ocean Strategy

Useful for: identifying market spaces where competition is irrelevant

Blue ocean thinking challenges you to create new market space rather than compete in existing space ("red ocean"). The Strategy Canvas tool:

1. List the factors your industry competes on (price, features, support quality, integration depth, etc.)
2. Plot where you and competitors invest on each factor (high/low)
3. Identify factors that could be: **Eliminated** (we invest in these but buyers don't value them), **Reduced** (we over-invest relative to value), **Raised** (we under-invest), **Created** (new factors the industry doesn't offer)

**PM application:** When looking for differentiation opportunities, the ERRC (Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create) grid is a useful exercise with your product team. It often surfaces: things you're building that nobody values (eliminate), and things customers need that nobody offers (create).

## Jobs-to-be-Done as strategy

JTBD is usually discussed as a research tool (see [Personas & JTBD](../04-product-discovery/personas-and-jtbd.md)). At the strategy level, it's a lens for identifying non-obvious competition.

People "hire" products for jobs. The competition for a job is often not who you think.

**Example:** The job "stay informed about what's happening in my industry" can be done by LinkedIn, industry newsletters, Slack communities, trade publications, and attending conferences. A B2B content tool competes with all of these for that job — not just other content tools.

**PM application:** Define the jobs your product is hired to do. Map all the ways customers could fire your product and hire an alternative. This reveals competition you're not otherwise watching.

## The Ansoff Matrix

Useful for: thinking about growth options systematically

|  | Existing Markets | New Markets |
|--|---|---|
| **Existing Products** | Market Penetration | Market Development |
| **New Products** | Product Development | Diversification |

- **Market Penetration:** Sell more of the current product to current markets — lowest risk
- **Product Development:** Build new products for current markets — moderate risk
- **Market Development:** Take current product to new markets/segments — moderate risk
- **Diversification:** New product + new market — highest risk

**PM application:** Most PMs are always in Product Development mode. The Ansoff matrix is useful for quarterly planning when you're weighing "expand the core product" vs. "adjacent product bet" vs. "new customer segment expansion." Make the risk profile of each option explicit.

## The Three Horizons Model

McKinsey's model for managing innovation portfolio across time:

- **Horizon 1:** Core business — existing products, existing customers. Focus: extend and defend.
- **Horizon 2:** Emerging businesses — products/markets that could be significant in 3–5 years. Focus: invest and scale.
- **Horizon 3:** Transformational bets — long-term ideas that might reshape the company. Focus: explore.

**PM application:** Use this to audit your roadmap. If 100% of your roadmap is H1 (incremental improvements to core product), you're optimizing for today at the expense of tomorrow. A healthy portfolio has ~70% H1, ~20% H2, ~10% H3.

## When frameworks mislead

Frameworks are analytical tools based on past patterns. They can mislead when:

- **The market is genuinely novel.** New technologies (AI, crypto, biotech) often don't fit existing frameworks cleanly.
- **You're using them as cover.** "The framework said X" is not a substitute for judgment.
- **You're optimizing for the framework, not the outcome.** A beautiful Strategy Canvas doesn't mean you've found a good strategy.

The best use of a framework: use it to generate questions, not answers. "Our Porter's analysis shows high buyer power — what are we doing about that?" is the right output. "Porter says we can't win here" is the wrong output.
