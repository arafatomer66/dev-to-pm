# Working with Leadership

The relationship between PM and leadership is different from most other PM relationships because the stakes are higher and the dynamics involve hierarchy in ways that other relationships don't.

## What leadership actually wants from PMs

**Good news and bad news equally.** Leadership that only hears good news has stopped receiving useful information. If you only escalate positive outcomes and quietly work around problems, leadership will eventually be surprised by something that should have been visible. The most valuable PMs are the ones who give accurate pictures, not optimistic ones.

**Confidence, not certainty.** Executives know the future is uncertain. They don't expect certainty. They do expect a PM who has thought through the key assumptions, stress-tested the hypothesis, and can speak to the risks. "I'm confident in this direction because X and Y, but we're watching Z" is leadership communication. "I don't know, we'll have to see" is not.

**Decisions, not options dumps.** When you bring a problem to leadership, bring a recommendation. "Here are three options and I can't decide" puts the work back on them. "I recommend option 2 for these reasons — but option 3 becomes the right call if X changes" respects their time and demonstrates PM judgment.

**Alignment on what matters.** Leadership needs to know the PM is optimizing for the right things. If there's ambiguity about what "success" means for your product, resolve it explicitly.

## Communicating upward

**The executive update format:**
1. What happened (facts, not spin)
2. What it means (your interpretation)
3. What you're doing about it
4. What you need from them (if anything)

Keep it short. Executives read many updates. A 500-word email gets read. A 2,000-word email gets skimmed.

**The strategy review format:** When presenting product strategy to leadership:
1. What we learned since last time (evidence)
2. What we're betting on this quarter (strategy, OKRs)
3. What's working and what isn't (honest assessment)
4. What we need to change or decide

**Cadence:** Monthly written updates plus quarterly strategy reviews is a minimum. More frequent if things are moving fast.

## Strategy disagreements with leadership

This happens. Leadership will sometimes have product opinions that conflict with your data and judgment.

**The wrong response:** Cave immediately to avoid conflict.

**The better response:**
1. Understand the reasoning behind their view — it may be based on information you don't have
2. Present your evidence and reasoning clearly and calmly
3. Make the tradeoffs explicit — "if we go in this direction, here's what we give up"
4. Propose a resolution: "Could we test this assumption before committing to the full investment?"

There are situations where the executive is simply right and your data was incomplete. There are situations where the executive is overriding a good decision based on gut feel. You need to be able to tell the difference and advocate appropriately in both cases.

**When to capitulate:** When leadership has information or context you don't have, or when the decision involves factors above your scope (investor relations, board expectations, M&A considerations). Ask enough questions to understand if this is the case.

**When to stand your ground:** When you have clear evidence that contradicts the direction and the stakes are high enough that it matters. Be respectful but clear: "I want to flag that our data suggests this won't work, and I want to make sure that's visible before we proceed."

## Managing resource conversations

At some point, you'll need to make the case for more engineering capacity, design time, or budget. This is a negotiation with leadership.

**Prepare like a business case:**
- What is the opportunity size? (revenue, users, strategic value)
- What investment is required? (headcount, time, cost)
- What is the return? (projected outcomes, timeframe)
- What is the cost of NOT investing? (competitive risk, technical debt cost, missed opportunity)
- What is the alternative use of that resource? (acknowledging the tradeoff)

Leadership approves resource requests based on ROI and strategic fit. "We need more engineers because we have a lot to build" doesn't work. "Adding one engineer to this team would accelerate our path to $X revenue by N months, which at our current growth rate is worth approximately $Y" works better.

## Navigating the HiPPO

The HiPPO (Highest Paid Person's Opinion) problem: a senior executive inserts a strong opinion that overrides the PM's data-informed judgment.

**Strategies:**
- **Frame as a hypothesis test:** "That's interesting — can we test that assumption before committing to it?" This respects their opinion without accepting it uncritically.
- **Show the cost:** "We could build that. Here's what we'd need to deprioritize to do it."
- **Document the decision:** When proceeding with a direction you disagree with, document the reasoning. This is not passive-aggressive — it's good practice. Future you (and future them) will want to know why a decision was made.
