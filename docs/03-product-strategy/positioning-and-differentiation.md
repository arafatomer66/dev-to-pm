# Positioning & Differentiation

Positioning is the answer to: "Why should a customer choose us over the alternatives?" It's not your tagline, your brand identity, or your marketing copy — those are downstream of positioning. Positioning is the strategic choice about where you compete and why you win there.

## The positioning statement

Geoffrey Moore's positioning statement template (from *Crossing the Chasm*) remains the most useful:

```
For [target customer]
who [has this need or faces this problem],
[product name] is a [product category]
that [key benefit / unique value].
Unlike [primary alternative],
our product [key differentiation].
```

**Example:**
> For mid-market finance teams who struggle to get clean data from disconnected ERP and CRM systems, Claros is a data integration platform that delivers audit-ready financial reports without manual reconciliation. Unlike enterprise-focused ETL tools that require months of implementation, Claros connects in days and is operated by finance teams, not IT.

This is not a tagline. It's an internal alignment document that answers: who do we serve, what problem, what's our category, what's different about us, and what's the specific alternative we're winning against?

Every major product decision should be evaluable against this statement: does this feature serve our target customer, does it reinforce our key differentiation, or is it scope creep?

## The four positioning traps

**The "better" trap:** "We're like Salesforce but better." Better is not a position — it's a hope. Better at what? For whom? Why can't Salesforce just get better too? Positioning needs to be specific enough that it would be difficult for a well-resourced competitor to simply match.

**The "all things to all people" trap:** When you're afraid to exclude any segment, you serve no segment particularly well. The most memorable products are remembered for something specific. Trying to be everything creates a product that's forgotten.

**The "feature list" trap:** Differentiation described as a feature list is fragile. Features get copied. Positioning built on features collapses when a competitor ships the same feature. The most durable positioning is built on something structural: a different business model, a different distribution channel, a different customer relationship.

**The "aspirational" trap:** "We're the AI-powered platform for the future of work" is positioning based on what you want to be, not what you are. Customers don't buy future positioning; they buy present value.

## Types of differentiation

Not all differentiation is equal. Listed roughly from least to most durable:

**Feature differentiation:** You have features they don't. Fragile — features get copied in 6–18 months.

**UX differentiation:** Your product is significantly easier to use. More durable — hard to copy quickly, but large competitors can eventually catch up.

**Integration/ecosystem differentiation:** You connect to 50 tools, they connect to 20. Moderately durable — has network effects; integrations take time to build.

**Customer segment differentiation:** You're built specifically for one segment (e.g., healthcare, legal, construction) with deep workflow understanding. Durable — requires domain expertise to replicate.

**Business model differentiation:** You charge per outcome, they charge per seat. Hard to copy without disrupting their own revenue model.

**Data/network effects differentiation:** Your product gets better as more customers use it. Very durable — competitors can't catch up without the same network.

**Distribution differentiation:** You have a channel (partner ecosystem, community, viral product-led growth) they can't easily replicate.

When assessing your differentiation, ask: "If a well-funded competitor decided to copy this, how long would it take?" Features: months. Customer segment + domain expertise: years.

## Positioning for different audiences

Your positioning needs to work for multiple audiences with different questions:

**End users:** "Will this make my job easier / my life better?"
**Buyers (in B2B):** "What business outcome does this produce? Is the ROI clear?"
**Technical evaluators:** "Does this integrate with what we have? Is it secure? Will it scale?"
**Executives:** "Why choose you over [known competitor]? What's the risk of switching?"

You don't need four different positioning statements — you need one core position that can be translated into each audience's language.

## Validating your positioning

Positioning is a hypothesis until it's validated in the market.

**Signals that your positioning is working:**
- Customers repeat your positioning back to you in their own words ("we chose you because you're the only one that...")
- Sales cycles for target customers are shorter than average
- Win rate against your primary alternative is high
- Customers who match your ICP have significantly better retention

**Signals that your positioning is broken:**
- Sales is selling to a different customer than you're building for
- Customers frequently ask for features that would move you in a different direction
- Win/loss analysis shows losses on dimensions you thought were strengths
- The team internally disagrees about who the product is for

When positioning is broken, don't patch it with marketing language. Fix the strategic misalignment between who you're building for, what you're building, and what you're saying to the market.

## Positioning vs. messaging vs. branding

These are different:

- **Positioning:** Strategic. Internal. Defines where and how you compete.
- **Messaging:** How positioning is expressed in words for each audience. Developed by PM + PMM together.
- **Branding:** Visual and emotional identity. Developed by brand/marketing. Downstream of positioning.

PMs own positioning. They work with Product Marketing Managers (PMMs) on messaging. They inform branding decisions but don't typically own them.
