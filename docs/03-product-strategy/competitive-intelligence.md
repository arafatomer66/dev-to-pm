# Competitive Intelligence

Competitive intelligence is not about copying competitors. It's about understanding the market well enough to make bets that competitors aren't making — and to avoid building what they've already commoditized.

## What to track and why

**Their product:** What can their product do? What can't it do? What do their users complain about? What do their users love?

**Their customers:** Who do they sell to? What segment are they winning? Are they moving up-market or down-market?

**Their go-to-market:** How do they acquire customers? What's their pricing model? How long is their sales cycle?

**Their strategy signals:** What are they building next? Where are they investing? What does their roadmap signal about where they think the market is going?

**Their weaknesses:** Where do they consistently lose deals? What do customers abandon them for? What can't they build without breaking what they have?

## Sources of competitive intelligence

### Public sources

**Their own website:** Product pages, pricing pages, case studies, blog posts, changelog. Their changelog is often the most honest view of where they're investing.

**Review sites:** G2, Capterra, Gartner Peer Insights. Read the 3-star reviews — they're the most informative. 5-star reviews are promotional; 1-star reviews are written in anger. 3-star reviews describe the real product honestly.

**Job postings:** A company hiring aggressively in a specific function signals where they're investing. Hiring 10 enterprise sales reps signals a move upmarket. Hiring ML engineers signals AI feature investment.

**Press coverage and blog posts:** Product announcements, funding rounds, partnership announcements. Take press releases with heavy skepticism, but they signal what they want the market to believe about them.

**LinkedIn:** Executive movement (who left, who they hired from), headcount growth by function, open roles.

**GitHub / developer communities:** If they have any open source presence, you can see what they're building. Developer communities (Discord, Slack, forums) often discuss product directions before they're public.

**Conference talks and webinars:** Product leaders often reveal roadmap directions in public talks, sometimes unintentionally.

### Customer-sourced intelligence

**Win/loss interviews:** The richest source. When you lose a deal, someone chose a competitor. Interview the decision-maker: what did they evaluate? What made them choose the other product? What were the specific differentiators?

**Churn interviews:** When a customer leaves for a competitor, this is a free competitive assessment. What specifically did the other product do better?

**Prospect discovery calls:** Prospects tell you what they're evaluating and what they're hearing in the market. This is real-time competitive intelligence.

**Customer advisory boards:** If you run a CAB, ask directly: what are they hearing about competitors from their peers?

### Sales team intelligence

Your sales team is in competitive deals every week. Create a structured way to capture what they're learning:

- Which competitors appear in deals most frequently?
- What objections come up when competing against each competitor?
- What features do prospects cite when they lean toward a competitor?
- What do you win on when you beat them?

A Slack channel `#competitive-intel` where sales reps share battle notes is cheap to run and valuable. A monthly review of win/loss patterns with the sales team gives you a real-time market signal.

## Win/loss analysis

Win/loss analysis is a structured process for understanding why you win and lose competitive deals.

**Basic framework:**

For every closed deal (won or lost), capture:
- Which competitors were evaluated?
- What was the final decision and primary reason?
- What were the top 3 decision criteria?
- What did the customer say about our strengths?
- What did the customer say about our weaknesses?

Analyze the patterns quarterly:
- What are we consistently winning on?
- What are we consistently losing on?
- Which competitor appears most often?
- Is there a deal size, industry, or segment where we consistently lose?

The output feeds both product strategy (where to invest to close capability gaps) and sales strategy (better battlecards, refined ICP).

## Reading the market without copying competitors

The goal of competitive intelligence is market understanding, not imitation.

**Bad outcome:** "Competitor X launched a feature. We should build it too." This is reactive, commodity-level competition.

**Good outcome:** "Competitor X launched a feature that solves problem Y. Their approach has limitations A and B. Our customers have the same problem Y but need a solution that addresses A and B. We can build a differentiated version."

Better still: "Competitor X is investing heavily in enterprise. That means they're pulling resources away from SMB. We should double down on SMB while they're distracted."

Competitive intelligence should lead you toward **asymmetric bets** — places where your strengths allow you to compete on ground that's unfavorable for your competitor.

## Competitive positioning for your product decisions

When you're making roadmap decisions, run each major initiative through a competitive filter:

1. **Does this close a gap?** (table stakes work — you need to be here to compete)
2. **Does this extend a lead?** (where you're already stronger, get more distance)
3. **Does this open new ground?** (a bet on a problem competitors haven't addressed)
4. **Does this defend against a threat?** (a competitor is moving into your market)

Table stakes work is necessary but not sufficient. If 80% of your roadmap is closing gaps, you're perpetually catching up. Winning products have a significant portion of roadmap on extending leads and opening new ground.

## What competitive intelligence is not

**Not a reason to panic.** A competitor launch is not a crisis. Stay focused on your strategy while absorbing the information.

**Not a reason to abandon your roadmap.** If a competitor launches a feature that's already on your roadmap for next quarter, do not drop everything to ship it faster. The quality of the decision matters more than the speed.

**Not a substitute for customer understanding.** The best competitive insight comes from customers, not from reading competitor websites. Customers will tell you what actually matters.
